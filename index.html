Japanese Internment
(1942-1946)
Overview In the Camp Impact About the Author

Impact

Psychological Impact while Internment

Shock, fear, and worry were common experience among internees. In the camp, internees have to endure the force of dislocation and abandonment of their properties and personal possessions.

Within the camp, the internees have to deal with the harsh condition of the camp. This include poor housing and food, lack of privacy, limited medical care, and low-quality education. A sense of hopelessness and inferior emerged under the authority of the camp. Tension increase when the U.S. government gave the internees royalty questionnaires.

The oldest internees, Issei, were community leader who were arrested after the bombing of Pearl Harbor and labeled as enemy aliens. They experienced intense stress from the abandonment of home, separation from friends and family. Some found that their children can no longer recognize them and some were denounced by the arrest and saw as a spy. Under the oppression of the camp and women were pay as much as men, male Issei feel powerless and lack a sense of a figure in family. Their children frequently ate with peers instead of families in the mass hall.

Nisei, the younger generation, were majority in their early adolescents. Their developmental stage as becoming an adult made them vulnerable to psychological effects. The government’s betrayal led them to depression and a sense of shame and humiliation.

Psychological Impact after Interment

Issei suffered the most after the Internment end because they were too old to start a new career. Nearly all of the Nisei never mentioned their experience at the camp, which a symptom of posttraumatic stress. Some have distant themselves from Japanese culture or anything that related to Japan. In contrast, some developed a distrust in white American and associated only with Japanese-Americans.

Nonetheless, the Nisei remained a low profile and fitted into American culture. This psychological defense impacted their children, Sansei. Sansei grew up hearing the internment from their parents.They experienced their parent’s unwillingness to discuss the internment and sensed that what happened was too painful to be discussed. The Sansei were also affected by their parents’ effort to blend into American culture and minimize anything related to Japan. As a result, they lost a sense of their own culture and language.
